Bill's research

The lifecycle of star clusters involves three stages: formation in clouds, evolution and migration through galaxies, and tidal dissolution into stellar streams. My research employs a variety of numerical methods to simulate all three stages in computers.

  • Stellar streams
  • Globular clusters and galaxy assembly
  • Gamma-ray bursts in distant galaxies

Formation of star clusters

Giant molecular clouds are the cradles of star clusters, in which most stars form, particularly in the early Universe. Understanding the modes and the efficiency with which stars and clusters form remains a central challenge in modern astrophysics.

Formation of massive star clusters in giant molecular clouds

Chen, Li, & Vogelsberger (2021)

We perform a suite of hydrodynamical simulations to investigate the effects of initial density profiles on the evolution of star clusters in giant molecular clouds using the moving-mesh code Arepo. We find that the uniform profile follows a "hierarchical" cluster formation mode, while the steep power-law profiles show an "accretion" dominated mode. These two cluster formation modes lead to different proprieties of the most massive clusters in giant molecular clouds.

Figure: "hierarchical" mode of cluster formation.

Simulating clustered star formation in cosmic morning

Chen et al. (in prep.)

We develop a continuous star cluster formation model in the cosmological hydrodynamic code ART to simulate the effects of clustered star formation during the cosmic morning (z = 3 - 10) on the observational properties of the earliest galaxies by the Hubble and James Webb Space Telescopes. This is a work in progress.

Figure: sketch of a star-froming cloud in simulations.

Stellar streams

When a progenitor star cluster or a dwarf galaxy is orbiting the host galaxy, tidal forces strip stars away from the L1 and L2 Lagrange points, forming a stellar stream with a leading arm and a trailing arm. These streams, stretching across wide sky areas, can reveal information not only about the host galaxy but also the progenitor itself.

Modelling stellar streams that emerge from globular clusters

Chen et al. (2025) Tutorial notebook

We develop a new particle spray algorithm to model the formation of globular cluster streams. The algorithm ejects stream tracer particles based on 6D phase space distributions calibrated to N-body simulations. It successfully matches the morphology and kinematics of simulated streams with higher accuracy than most existing particle spray methods. The new algorithm is implemented in galactic dynamics codes agama, gala, galax, and galpy for public use.

Figure: positions and velocity vectors of escaped stream particles near the Lagrange points.

Measuring mass loss rates of globular clusters using stellar streams

Chen, Li, & Gnedin (2025) Chen, Gnedin, & Price-Whelan (2025, in prep.)

By comparing the densities of streams recently discovered by Gaia DR3 with mock streams generated using our new particle spray algorithm, we present the first direct measurement of mass loss rates for 12 Galactic globular clusters. We further explore correlations between the mass loss rate and key GC properties, confirming theoretical predictions from N-body simulations.

Figure: mass loss rates of 12 Galactic globular clusters plotted against cluster mass.

The most realistic mock catalog of globular cluster streams

Holm-Hansen, Chen, & Gnedin (2025) Catalog

We use a comprehensive model of globular cluster formation and disruption, coupled with our new particle spray algorithm, to generate the most realistic mock catalog of globular cluster streams in simulated Milky Way-like galaxies.

Figure: map of mock stream stars in the Galactic coordinate system with G < 20.

Automatic detection method for globular cluster streams

Chen et al. (2025, submitted) GitHub repo

We develop StarStream, a physics-based method to automatically detect stellar streams around globular clusters by constructing a mixture model containing a stream model and background. The stream model is based on our new particle spray algorithm, without assuming the shape and orientation of streams. The new method shows 65% completeness and purity when tested on mock data at high Galactic latitudes (|b|>30°).

Figure: an example stream revealed by the new method.

New streams in the Milky Way

Chen, Gnedin, & Price-Whelan (2025, in prep.) Data release

Applying StarStream to Gaia DR3, we successfully detect 87 globular cluster streams in the Milky Way. Many streams significantly deviate the progenitor's orbit or have "irregular" morphology, contradicting the past visual expectation. The quantified detection quality enables us to measure unbiased cluster mass loss rates for 34 high-quality detections. We find several low-mass, large-size clusters with enhanced mass loss rates, likely driven by black holes comprising around 20% of cluster mass.

Figure: we discover NGC 4147's short, "blobby" stream.

Globular clusters and galaxy assembly

Globular clusters are old massive star clusters that serve as "fossils" of galaxy formation. Most galaxies host at least one globular cluster, with the Milky Way containing approximately 170. These clusters provide valuable insights for unveiling the hierarchical assembly of their host galaxy.

Modelling the chemistry and kinematics of globular clusters

Chen & Gnedin (2022)

The advent of the Gaia mission has enabled detailed chemical and kinematic studies of the Galactic globular clusters and revolutionized our understanding of the connections between globular cluster properties and galaxy assembly. By assigning globular clusters to particles in the IllustrisTNG simulation based on age and location, we develop a new globular cluster formation model with chemical, spatial, and kinematic information. The model successfully reproduces the radial distribution and various kinematic properties of the Galactic globular cluster system.

Figure: effective radii of globular cluster systems vs total mass of host halos.

Formation of globular clusters in local group satellite galaxies

Chen & Gnedin (2023)

We validate the near-linear globular cluster mass-halo mass correlation down to Mh ~ 108 Msun, where the majority of dwarf galaxies do not host any cluster, using our globular cluster formation model. By studying two Fornax-like satellites in the simulations, we reproduce the radial profile of globular clusters in Fornax and show that observational samples can be notably biased by incompleteness below detection limit and at large radii.

Figure: near-linear globular cluster mass-halo mass correlation.

Reconstructing galaxy assembly history using globular clusters

Chen & Gnedin (2024a) Chen & Gnedin (2024b) Catalog

We generate a mock catalog of globular clusters reproducing the total mass, mass function, metallicity distribution, radial profile, and velocity dispersion of the Galactic globular cluster systems by applying our globular cluster formation model to simulated Milky Way-like galaxies. Based on this catalog, we examine various clustering methods to categorize the progenitors of globular clusters, using their age, chemical, and kinematic properties, and subsequently apply the same methods and properties to Galactic clusters.

Gamma-ray bursts

Gamma-ray bursts are one of the most energetic events that happen in distant galaxies. They offer a unique opportunity to examine the fundamental laws of physics.

Pre-burst stage of gamma-ray bursts

Chen & Ma (2021)

Based on the cosmic light speed variation, we find a novel pre-burst stage for gamma-ray bursts. We also employ a primary clustering method of machine learning to classify this stage with the data from the Fermi telescope. The work was completed in 2019 but was accepted for publication in 2021 due to the COVID-19 pandemic.
